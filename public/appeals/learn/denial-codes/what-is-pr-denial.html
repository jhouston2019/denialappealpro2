Denial Codes

What Is a PR Denial?

PR shifts dollars to the patient — deductible, copay, coinsurance — and knowing which PR codes are collectible versus which signal a deniable underlying claim saves your team hours of misdirected appeals.

Definition

A Patient Responsibility denial — the amount the patient owes after insurance processes the claim (deductible, copay, coinsurance).

What a PR Denial Means

PR is a claim adjustment group code standing for Patient Responsibility. It appears on remittance advices before CARC numbers — PR-1, PR-2, PR-3 — and indicates the adjusted amount belongs to the patient, not the provider or payer. PR-1 maps to deductible, PR-2 to coinsurance, and PR-3 to copay. When paired with denial CARC codes like PR-96, the patient may owe the full non-covered charge. PR adjustments confirm the claim was processed and the insurance portion was calculated — the remaining balance is the patient's contractual cost-sharing under their plan.

Why PR Denials Matter

PR adjustments are not denials in the traditional sense — they often mean the claim was paid and the patient owes their share. Misinterpreting PR-1 as a denial and submitting an appeal wastes staff time and delays patient billing. Conversely, treating a PR-96 non-covered charge as simple patient billing when the service was actually covered under the plan means lost revenue. Patient responsibility collections account for an estimated 30% to 35% of provider revenue, making accurate PR code interpretation critical for cash flow. High-deductible plans have pushed average patient responsibility per claim above $150 for commercial members.

How PR Denials Affect Your Claim

PR adjustments reduce the payer's payment and create a patient balance. On a $400 claim with $320 allowed, PR-2 coinsurance at 20% means the payer pays $256 and the patient owes $64. The claim is successfully processed — your action is patient billing, not an appeal. When PR appears with a denial CARC code and zero insurance payment, the entire charge may shift to the patient. This is appropriate for genuinely non-covered services with a signed waiver, but inappropriate when the payer incorrectly denied a covered service. Always check whether insurance paid anything before sending the patient a statement for the full amount.

PR-1, PR-2, and PR-3 on a paid claim mean collect from the patient — do not appeal. PR paired with a denial CARC code and $0 insurance payment means investigate before billing the patient.

What to Do About a PR Denial

Check whether the claim was paid or denied. If paid with PR cost-sharing, post the patient balance and send a statement showing the allowed amount, insurance payment, and patient responsibility. Verify the deductible and coinsurance calculations against the patient's plan benefits. If PR-96 or another denial CARC appears with zero insurance payment, review whether the service is truly non-covered or whether an appeal to the payer should precede patient billing. For PR adjustments you believe are incorrect — wrong deductible applied, out-of-network rate when provider is in-network — contact payer provider services before billing the patient. Frequently Asked Questions

What does PR mean on an EOB?

PR stands for Patient Responsibility. It indicates the adjusted amount is owed by the patient — typically a deductible, copay, coinsurance, or non-covered service charge.

Is a PR denial something I should appeal?

PR-1, PR-2, and PR-3 for deductible, coinsurance, and copay are not appealable to the payer — they are legitimate patient cost-sharing. PR adjustments paired with denial CARC codes like 96 or 50 may warrant appealing the underlying denial.

What are the common PR CARC codes?

PR-1 is deductible, PR-2 is coinsurance, PR-3 is copay, and PR-96 is non-covered charge passed to the patient. Each maps to a specific patient billing scenario.

When should I bill the patient after a PR adjustment?

Bill the patient when the PR adjustment reflects legitimate cost-sharing on a paid claim, or when a non-covered service waiver was signed before treatment. Verify the insurance portion paid before sending a patient statement.
