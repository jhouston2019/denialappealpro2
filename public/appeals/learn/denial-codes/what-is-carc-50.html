Denial Codes

What Is CARC 50?

CARC 50 is the payer telling you they disagree with your clinical judgment — the service may be covered under the plan, but they do not believe it was necessary for this patient.

Definition

Payment denied for lack of medical necessity — the payer determined the service was not medically required for the patient's condition.

What CARC 50 Means

CARC code 50 indicates that the payer has determined the billed service is not deemed a medical necessity for the patient's condition. Unlike a non-covered benefit denial, CARC 50 assumes the service type can be covered under the plan — the payer simply disagrees that it was appropriate for this specific patient, diagnosis, or clinical scenario. The denial typically appears with a CO group code, meaning the provider cannot balance-bill the patient for the denied amount. A RARC code often accompanies CARC 50, referencing the payer's medical policy or clinical criteria that were not satisfied.

Why CARC 50 Matters

Medical necessity denials are among the most expensive and contentious in revenue cycle management. CARC 50 appears across every specialty — from advanced imaging and genetic testing to physical therapy and DME — and individual denials can range from $500 to $15,000 per claim. Payers apply proprietary clinical criteria and utilization management rules that vary by plan, making CARC 50 one of the hardest denials to prevent at the front end. Yet it remains one of the most winnable on appeal: documented medical necessity appeals succeed 40% to 65% of the time when the clinical record clearly supports the service.

How CARC 50 Affects Your Claim

A CARC 50 denial zeroes out or significantly reduces payment on the affected line item. The payer's medical director or automated clinical review system evaluated your submitted diagnosis codes, clinical notes, and possibly prior treatment history against their coverage policy. If the documentation did not explicitly connect the patient's condition to the medical rationale for the service, the claim fails review. CARC 50 on one line does not always mean the entire claim is denied — other lines may pay normally. Check whether the payer offered a peer-to-peer review option before formal appeal, as overturn rates improve when the treating physician speaks directly with the payer's medical director.

CARC 50 appeals win when your letter addresses the payer's specific coverage criteria point by point — not when you simply restate that the service was ordered. Obtain the payer's medical policy and map your documentation to each requirement.

What to Do About CARC 50

Request the payer's medical policy for the denied service and identify which coverage criteria were not met. Obtain a letter of medical necessity from the treating physician that explicitly addresses each criterion. Attach relevant clinical notes, diagnostic results, and peer-reviewed literature supporting the treatment choice. Request a peer-to-peer review if available — many payers require it before accepting a formal appeal. Submit your first-level appeal within 30 to 60 days of the denial date. Frequently Asked Questions

What does CARC 50 mean on an EOB?

CARC 50 means the payer determined the service is not medically necessary for the patient's diagnosis or condition. Payment is denied because the payer's clinical criteria for coverage were not met.

Is CARC 50 the same as a non-covered benefit?

No. CARC 50 is a medical necessity denial — the service could be covered but the payer disagrees it was appropriate for this patient. CARC 96 indicates the service is excluded from the plan entirely.

What documentation overturns a CARC 50 denial?

Clinical notes linking the diagnosis to the service, peer-reviewed literature, letter of medical necessity from the treating physician, and the payer's own coverage policy showing the service meets their criteria.

What is the appeal success rate for CARC 50?

Medical necessity appeals overturn approximately 40% to 65% of denials when supported by strong clinical documentation and a physician letter addressing the payer's specific coverage criteria.
