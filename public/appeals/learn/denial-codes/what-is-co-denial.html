Denial Codes

What Is a CO Denial?

CO means Contractual Obligation — the adjusted dollars are yours to absorb under your payer agreement, and billing the patient for that amount is off the table.

Definition

A Contractual Obligation denial — an adjustment the provider is contractually prohibited from billing to the patient.

What a CO Denial Means

CO is a claim adjustment group code that stands for Contractual Obligation. It appears before the CARC number on your remittance advice — for example, CO-97 or CO-45 — and tells you the adjusted amount is the provider's financial responsibility under the payer contract. The patient cannot be billed for CO adjustments. This group code applies to denials, fee schedule reductions, bundling edits, and any adjustment where the provider agreed — explicitly or by participating in the payer network — to accept the payer's allowed amount as payment in full.

Why CO Denials Matter

CO is the most common adjustment group code on remittance advices, appearing on an estimated 70% of all claim adjustments. Understanding CO prevents two costly mistakes: billing patients for amounts you cannot collect from them, and writing off denials that are actually appealable. A CO-45 fee schedule reduction is often expected and requires no action. A CO-97 bundling denial or CO-50 medical necessity denial represents recoverable revenue if you appeal with the right documentation. Misclassifying a CO denial as unrecoverable when the underlying CARC code is appealable costs practices thousands of dollars monthly.

How CO Denials Affect Your Claim

Every CO adjustment reduces your expected reimbursement by the adjusted amount. On a $500 line item with a CO-97 bundling denial, you receive $0 and cannot shift the loss to the patient. On a $500 line with CO-45, the payer may pay $320 at contracted rates and the remaining $180 is your contractual write-off — not a denial requiring appeal. CO denials appear alongside specific CARC codes that explain the reason. Your response depends entirely on which CARC code follows the CO prefix. Multiple CO adjustments can appear on a single claim line, each with a different CARC code requiring a separate response strategy.

CO tells you who owes the money — you, not the patient. The CARC number after CO tells you why. Always read both before deciding to appeal, rebill, or write off.

What to Do About a CO Denial

Identify the CARC code paired with the CO group code. For CO-45 fee schedule adjustments, verify the allowed amount matches your contract — dispute if it does not. For CO-97, CO-50, CO-29, or CO-197, build an appeal targeting the specific CARC denial reason. Never bill the patient for the CO adjustment amount. Track CO denials by CARC code in your denial management system to identify patterns — if CO-97 denials spike after a coding update, fix the root cause rather than appealing individually. Frequently Asked Questions

What does CO mean on a denial code?

CO stands for Contractual Obligation. It means the adjusted amount is the provider's responsibility under their payer contract and cannot be billed to the patient.

Can I bill the patient for a CO denial?

No. CO adjustments are contractually prohibited from being passed to the patient. Billing a patient for a CO amount may violate your payer agreement and No Surprises Act provisions.

What is the most common CO denial code?

CO-45 is the most common contractual adjustment, representing a charge exceeding the payer's fee schedule or maximum allowable. CO-97 for bundling and CO-50 for medical necessity are also frequent.

How do I recover payment on a CO denial?

Appeal the underlying CARC code with supporting documentation, request a corrected claim if the denial was caused by a billing error, or negotiate the fee schedule if the allowed amount is below your contract rate.
