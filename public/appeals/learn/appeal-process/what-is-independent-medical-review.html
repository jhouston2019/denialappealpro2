Appeal Process

What Is Independent Medical Review (IMR)?

When a payer's own reviewers deny medical necessity on a CARC 50 claim, IMR puts your records in front of an unaffiliated physician — and overturn rates at this stage reach 60% for cases with strong clinical documentation.

Definition

A review of a medical necessity denial by an independent physician who has no relationship with the payer.

What Independent Medical Review Means

Independent Medical Review is a formal clinical evaluation of a denied claim performed by a physician who is not employed by, contracted with, or financially tied to the insurance company. The reviewing doctor examines the patient's medical records, the payer's denial rationale, and applicable coverage criteria to determine whether the service was medically necessary. IMR is a subset of external review focused specifically on clinical judgment disputes.

States like California have established dedicated IMR programs administered through the Department of Managed Health Care, while other states integrate IMR into broader external review processes managed by Independent Review Organizations.

Why IMR Matters

Medical necessity denials are among the most common and highest-dollar denials in healthcare billing. Payers apply automated edits and internal clinical guidelines that may not account for the full patient picture. IMR introduces a board-certified physician in the relevant specialty who evaluates the case on clinical merits alone. For providers facing repeated CARC 50 denials from the same payer, IMR is often the most effective escalation path.

IMR decisions are binding on the insurer in participating states. A favorable IMR ruling requires the payer to authorize or pay for the denied service — not merely reconsider it.

How IMR Affects Your Claim

Once IMR is requested, the case file goes to an assigned independent physician who reviews all submitted documentation. The reviewer does not communicate with the payer's internal team during evaluation. A decision is issued within 30 to 45 days for standard cases. If the IMR physician overturns the denial, the payer must reprocess the claim or authorize the service retroactively. If upheld, remaining options depend on state law and may include further external review or regulatory complaints.

Submit complete clinical records with your IMR request. The independent reviewer can only evaluate what you provide — incomplete records lead to uphold decisions even when the treatment was clinically appropriate.

What to Do When Requesting IMR

Confirm IMR eligibility for your denial type and state. Complete all required internal appeal levels first unless state law allows concurrent IMR. Compile comprehensive clinical documentation — progress notes, diagnostic results, treatment history, and physician attestation. Submit the IMR request through your state insurance department or the payer's designated IMR process before the deadline. Include a cover letter summarizing why the clinical evidence supports medical necessity. Track the case number and expected decision date.

Frequently Asked Questions

What types of denials qualify for IMR?

IMR typically applies to medical necessity denials, experimental or investigational treatment denials, and certain coverage disputes. Eligibility varies by state — California, for example, requires IMR for specific HMO denials after internal appeals are exhausted.

How is IMR different from a peer-to-peer review?

Peer-to-peer review is a live conversation between your treating physician and the payer's medical director during the internal appeal process. IMR is a formal document review by an independent physician with no payer affiliation, conducted after internal appeals fail.

Is there a cost to request IMR?

In most states, IMR is free for the patient and provider. The insurer pays the Independent Review Organization for the review. Some states allow patients to submit IMR requests directly through the state insurance department website.

How long does IMR take?

Standard IMR decisions are issued within 30 to 45 days. Expedited IMR for urgent treatment situations may be completed within 72 hours. The reviewing physician evaluates all submitted medical records without contacting the treating provider unless additional information is needed.
