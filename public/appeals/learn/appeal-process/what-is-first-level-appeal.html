Appeal Process

What Is a First-Level Appeal?

The first-level appeal is where every denied claim fight begins — a written request to the payer's internal review team that must land within 60 to 180 days or the denial becomes permanent.

Definition

The initial internal review request submitted directly to the insurance payer after a denial — the first step in the formal appeal process.

What a First-Level Appeal Means

A first-level appeal is the opening move in the formal dispute process between a provider or patient and an insurance payer. After receiving a denial on a remittance advice or EOB, you submit a written appeal letter to the payer requesting reconsideration. This triggers an internal review by the payer's appeals department — a team separate from initial claims processing in most organizations, though still employed by the same insurer.

The first-level appeal must directly address the stated denial reason. If the payer denied for medical necessity, your letter proves necessity. If they denied for missing authorization, you attach the authorization number and effective dates. Generic appeals that do not map evidence to the specific CARC code rarely succeed at any level.

Why the First-Level Appeal Matters

You cannot skip to external review or second-level appeal without completing first-level review in most cases. This step establishes your formal record of disagreement with the payer's decision. Payers track first-level appeal outcomes as part of their regulatory reporting, and a well-documented first-level submission creates the foundation for escalation if needed.

First-level appeals also resolve a meaningful share of denials on their own. Many denials result from data entry errors, missing attachments, or automated edit mistakes that a targeted first-level letter with corrected information overturns without further escalation.

How a First-Level Appeal Affects Your Claim

Once submitted, the claim enters appeal pending status. The payer has a defined response window — typically 30 days for urgent cases and up to 60 days for standard commercial appeals. During review, the payer may request additional documentation or offer a peer-to-peer review opportunity. If approved, the claim reprocesses and payment posts to a subsequent remittance. If denied, you receive a formal denial letter with the reason and instructions for filing a second-level appeal, usually with a shorter deadline of 60 days or less.

Attach every document your letter references. First-level appeals are denied more often for incomplete submissions than for weak arguments — a letter citing progress notes without attaching them gives the reviewer no evidence to overturn the denial.

What to Do When Filing a First-Level Appeal

Identify the CARC code and denial reason from your remittance advice. Draft a letter that restates the denial, presents your counter-argument with specific claim details, and lists every attachment. Submit through the payer's required channel and retain proof of submission with date and reference number. Calendar the expected response date plus the second-level deadline in case the first appeal fails. Track the outcome in your denial management system so patterns by payer and denial type become visible over time.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is a first-level appeal different from a second-level appeal?

A first-level appeal is reviewed by the payer's initial appeals team — often the same department that processed the original claim. A second-level appeal goes to a different or more senior review team with fresh eyes on the case. You must complete first-level review before escalating unless the payer allows simultaneous external review in specific situations.

How long does a first-level appeal take?

Commercial payers typically respond to first-level appeals within 30 to 60 days. Medicare redeterminations follow a similar timeline. Urgent or expedited appeals for ongoing treatment may receive faster responses under regulatory requirements.

Can I submit a first-level appeal online?

Most major payers accept first-level appeals through their provider portal, by fax, or by mail. Check the payer's provider manual for the required submission method. Some payers reject appeals submitted through the wrong channel, so confirm before sending.

What happens if my first-level appeal is denied?

If your first-level appeal is denied, you receive a denial letter with the reason and instructions for filing a second-level appeal. Note the new deadline — it is often shorter than the first-level window. Gather any additional documentation and escalate before that deadline expires.
