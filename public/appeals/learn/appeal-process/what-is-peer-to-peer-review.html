Appeal Process

What Is Peer-to-Peer Review?

A 15-minute phone call between your physician and the payer's medical director resolves more than 70% of medical necessity denials — faster and cheaper than a written appeal alone.

Definition

A direct conversation between the treating physician and the payer's medical director to discuss a medical necessity denial before a formal appeal is filed.

What Peer-to-Peer Review Means

Peer-to-peer review is a real-time clinical discussion between the provider who treated the patient and the insurance company's medical director. It occurs by phone, typically lasting 10 to 20 minutes, and focuses on why the service was ordered and whether it meets the payer's medical necessity criteria. Unlike written appeals where billing staff translate clinical decisions into letters, peer-to-peer puts the treating physician directly in conversation with the payer's clinical reviewer.

Most major commercial payers and Medicare Advantage plans offer peer-to-peer review for medical necessity denials, prior authorization denials, and high-dollar service disputes.

Why Peer-to-Peer Review Matters

Written appeals depend on documentation the payer may not fully interpret. Peer-to-peer allows the treating physician to explain clinical context that does not fit neatly into progress note formats — symptom progression, failed conservative treatment, comorbidities affecting the standard protocol. This direct clinical conversation resolves denials that written appeals alone cannot, often within 24 to 72 hours instead of 30 to 60 days.

For practices where physicians resist involvement in billing disputes, peer-to-peer review is the highest-ROI use of physician time in the denial management process.

How Peer-to-Peer Review Affects Your Claim

If the payer's medical director agrees with the treating physician's clinical rationale during the call, the denial is overturned and the claim reprocesses — sometimes the same day. If the payer upholds the denial, the conversation still creates a documented record of clinical disagreement that strengthens subsequent written appeals and independent medical review requests. The peer-to-peer outcome is noted in the payer's system and referenced in any subsequent appeal letters.

The treating physician must participate — not billing staff. Payer medical directors expect clinical dialogue and will uphold the denial if the person on the call cannot discuss diagnosis, treatment rationale, and patient findings.

What to Do When Requesting Peer-to-Peer Review

Request peer-to-peer as soon as you receive a medical necessity or authorization denial — do not wait for a written appeal to fail. Have the treating physician review the patient's chart, the denial reason, and the payer's medical policy before the call. During the conversation, the physician should state the diagnosis, explain why the service was ordered, reference specific clinical findings, and address the payer's stated denial reason directly. Document the call outcome including the payer representative's name, date, and decision. Follow up with a written appeal if the peer-to-peer does not result in overturn.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I schedule a peer-to-peer review?

Contact the payer's provider services or appeals department and request a peer-to-peer review for the denied claim. Provide the claim number, patient name, and date of service. The payer schedules a phone call between your treating physician and their medical director, typically within 24 to 72 hours for urgent cases.

Does peer-to-peer review replace a written appeal?

No. Peer-to-peer review supplements the written appeal process but does not replace it in most cases. File your written appeal before or concurrently with the peer-to-peer request to preserve your appeal deadline. Some payers overturn denials during peer-to-peer and reprocess without requiring a formal letter.

Who should participate in the peer-to-peer call?

The treating physician who ordered the service should participate — not a billing staff member or office manager. The payer's medical director expects to discuss clinical findings, diagnosis rationale, and treatment decisions with a licensed provider who has direct knowledge of the patient's case.

What is the success rate of peer-to-peer reviews?

When the treating physician participates and presents clinical rationale directly, peer-to-peer reviews overturn denials at rates exceeding 70% for medical necessity cases. Success drops significantly when billing staff attempt the call without physician involvement.
