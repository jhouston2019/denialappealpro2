Appeal Process

What Is Appeal Success Rate?

Between 40% and 75% of formally appealed denials are overturned industry-wide — yet most practices write off denials without appealing because they assume the payer's decision is final.

Definition

The percentage of denied claims that are overturned or paid after a formal appeal is submitted — industry benchmarks range from 40% to 75% depending on denial type.

What Appeal Success Rate Means

Appeal success rate measures how often formal appeals result in payment recovery. It is calculated by dividing the number of overturned appeals by the total number of appeals submitted over a given period. The metric can be tracked at the claim level, by CARC code, by payer, or by appeal level. Industry benchmarks published by revenue cycle research firms consistently show that 40% to 75% of appealed denials are overturned — a range wide enough to reflect real differences in denial type, documentation quality, and escalation depth.

Success rate is not a fixed property of the payer. It varies based on how well the provider builds and submits the appeal.

Why Appeal Success Rate Matters

Denial write-off decisions are often made without knowing the statistical likelihood of recovery. A billing team that writes off every CARC 50 denial assumes a 0% success rate when the industry average for medical necessity appeals with clinical documentation exceeds 60%. Tracking appeal success rate by denial type transforms denial management from reactive write-off to data-driven revenue recovery.

Success rate data also reveals which payers, service lines, and denial codes deserve the most appeal effort — and which are genuinely low-probability bets.

How Appeal Success Rate Affects Your Claim Strategy

A practice with a 55% appeal success rate on $200,000 in annual denials recovers $110,000 that would otherwise write off. Improving that rate to 65% through targeted templates, better documentation, and consistent escalation adds $20,000 in recovered revenue without increasing appeal volume. Success rate by appeal level guides escalation decisions — if first-level appeals succeed at 35% but external review succeeds at 55%, the data supports pushing more claims through the full escalation path rather than stopping after first-level denial.

Most practices under-appeal because they overestimate payer accuracy. Industry data shows at least 40% of appealed denials are overturned — meaning the payer's initial denial was wrong or incomplete nearly half the time.

What to Do to Improve Your Appeal Success Rate

Track overturn outcomes by CARC code, payer, and appeal level in your denial management system. Build CARC-specific appeal templates with the documentation checklist each denial type requires. Escalate beyond first level when the dollar amount justifies the effort and clinical documentation supports the case. Request peer-to-peer review for medical necessity denials before or alongside written appeals. Measure success rate monthly and identify the denial types and payers where your rate falls below industry benchmarks — those gaps represent your highest-ROI improvement opportunities.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good appeal success rate for a medical practice?

A well-managed denial program targets a 50% to 65% overturn rate across all denial types. Authorization and medical necessity appeals often exceed 60% when supported by strong documentation. Timely filing appeals succeed at lower rates unless submission proof is available.

Which denial types have the highest appeal success rates?

Medical necessity denials with strong clinical documentation, prior authorization denials where authorization was actually obtained, and bundling denials with correct modifier usage tend to have the highest overturn rates — often exceeding 65% at external review.

Why do many practices have low appeal success rates?

Low success rates typically stem from generic appeal letters that do not address the specific CARC code, missing attachments, missed deadlines, and failure to escalate beyond first-level review. Practices that map each denial type to a targeted template and track outcomes by payer consistently outperform those that use one generic letter for all denials.

Does appeal level affect success rate?

Yes. Success rates generally increase at higher appeal levels. External review and independent medical review overturn 50% or more of denials in many states. First-level appeals succeed at lower rates because they are reviewed by the same payer infrastructure that issued the original denial.
