Appeal Process

What Is an Insurance Appeal?

When a payer denies or reduces payment on a $1,200 claim, an insurance appeal is your formal written argument asking them to reconsider — the single most important tool revenue cycle teams have to recover denied revenue.

Definition

A formal written request to an insurance payer to reconsider and reverse a denied or reduced claim payment.

What an Insurance Appeal Means

An insurance appeal is the structured process providers and patients use to challenge a payer's decision on a claim. Unlike a phone call to customer service, an appeal is a documented request that triggers a formal review under the payer's internal appeals policy or applicable state and federal regulations. The appeal letter restates the denial reason, presents counter-evidence, and asks the payer to reprocess the claim at the originally billed or contracted amount.

Appeals exist at multiple levels — first-level internal review, second-level internal review, and external independent review — each with its own deadline and escalation path. Understanding which level you are filing determines what documentation to attach and how long the payer has to respond.

Why Insurance Appeals Matter

Denials are not final decisions. Industry estimates suggest that roughly 17% of in-network claims are denied on first pass, yet a significant share of those denials are overturned when appealed with the right documentation. For a practice processing 500 claims per month, even a modest denial rate translates to tens of thousands of dollars in revenue at risk every quarter. Appeals convert denials back into collectible revenue without re-performing the service.

Payers also use denials as a cost-control mechanism. Without appeals, incorrect denials — wrong CARC codes, missing authorization data, or automated edit errors — would permanently reduce provider reimbursement. The appeal process is the check against those errors.

How an Insurance Appeal Affects Your Claim

Filing an appeal puts the claim into a pending status while the payer reviews your submission. During review, the payer may request additional records, schedule a peer-to-peer conversation between physicians, or issue a decision within 30 to 60 days depending on plan type. A successful appeal results in reprocessing: the claim is paid, partially paid, or adjusted to reflect corrected information. An unsuccessful appeal at one level opens the path to the next level of review.

Every appeal must be submitted before the payer's deadline. A well-written letter submitted one day late is treated the same as no appeal at all — the denial stands and the revenue is lost.

What to Do When You Need to Appeal

Start by reading the remittance advice or denial letter to identify the CARC code and denial reason. Gather supporting documentation — clinical notes, authorization numbers, proof of timely submission — that directly addresses that reason. Draft a letter that maps your evidence to the payer's stated basis for denial, not a generic request to pay the claim. Submit through the payer's required channel — portal, fax, or mail — and track the reference number and response deadline. If the first-level appeal is denied, escalate to the next level before that deadline expires as well.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who can file an insurance appeal?

Either the healthcare provider or the patient can file an insurance appeal. Providers typically appeal on behalf of the practice when a claim is denied or underpaid. Patients can appeal when they receive a denial on an Explanation of Benefits or when a service is not covered as expected.

How long do I have to file an insurance appeal?

Appeal deadlines vary by payer and plan type. Commercial insurers often allow 60 to 180 days from the denial date. Medicare allows 120 days for redetermination. Missing the deadline typically closes the appeal path entirely, so check your remittance advice or denial letter for the exact filing window.

What should an insurance appeal letter include?

A strong appeal letter includes claim identification details, a clear statement of the denial reason, a targeted rebuttal with supporting documentation, references to payer policy or coverage criteria when applicable, and a formal request for reprocessing. Attach clinical records, authorization proof, or billing corrections as needed.

How successful are insurance appeals?

Industry data shows that 40% to 75% of appealed denials are overturned or paid, depending on denial type and appeal level. Medical necessity and authorization denials tend to have higher overturn rates when supported by strong clinical documentation.
