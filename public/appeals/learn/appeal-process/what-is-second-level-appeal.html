Appeal Process

What Is a Second-Level Appeal?

When a first-level appeal fails on a $3,500 denied claim, the second-level appeal puts your case in front of a different reviewer — and data shows overturn rates climb 15 to 20 points at this stage when new clinical evidence is added.

Definition

A follow-up appeal submitted when the first-level appeal is denied — reviewed by a different or more senior team at the payer.

What a Second-Level Appeal Means

A second-level appeal is the escalation step after an insurer upholds its denial at first-level review. You are asking a different decision-maker — not the same reviewer who denied your initial appeal — to evaluate the claim with fresh scrutiny. For Medicare beneficiaries and providers, this stage is called reconsideration and is handled by a Qualified Independent Contractor. Commercial payers route second-level appeals to senior medical directors or dedicated escalation teams.

The second-level submission should not be a copy-paste of your first-level letter. It must respond to the specific reasons cited in the first-level denial and introduce any additional evidence that strengthens your position.

Why Second-Level Appeals Matter

First-level denials are not the final word. Many payers initially uphold denials as a matter of process, expecting providers to escalate only serious cases. Second-level review is where clinical complexity gets more attention — especially for high-dollar claims involving surgery, imaging, DME, or specialty drugs. Practices that abandon claims after first-level denial leave recoverable revenue on the table.

Regulatory frameworks in most states require insurers to offer at least two internal appeal levels before external review becomes available. Skipping this step closes the path to independent review.

How a Second-Level Appeal Affects Your Claim

Submitting a second-level appeal resets the review clock. Payers typically respond within 30 to 60 days, though Medicare reconsideration can take longer. A favorable decision results in claim reprocessing and payment on a future remittance advice. An unfavorable decision generates a new denial letter with instructions for external or independent review — the next escalation path with its own deadline, often 60 days or less from the second-level denial date.

Read the first-level denial letter before writing your second-level appeal. Address every reason the payer cited for upholding the denial — ignoring a single objection gives them grounds to deny again without engaging your argument.

What to Do When Escalating to Second Level

Obtain the first-level denial letter and identify every stated reason for upholding the denial. Gather new supporting documentation — physician attestation, additional clinical records, literature citations, or corrected claim information. Draft a letter that references your first-level submission date, responds point-by-point to the denial reasons, and presents the new evidence. Submit before the deadline listed on the denial letter and track the reference number. If the claim involves medical necessity, request a peer-to-peer review concurrently if the payer allows it.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I file a second-level appeal?

File a second-level appeal when you receive a formal denial of your first-level appeal and still believe the payer's decision is incorrect. Review the denial reason carefully — your second-level submission should address any new objections the payer raised, not simply resubmit the same letter.

Who reviews a second-level appeal?

Second-level appeals are reviewed by a different team or more senior reviewer than the first-level appeal. For Medicare, this is reconsideration by a Qualified Independent Contractor. For commercial plans, it is typically an internal escalation unit with authority to overturn the initial appeals decision.

What is the deadline for a second-level appeal?

Second-level appeal deadlines are often shorter than first-level windows — commonly 60 days from the first-level denial date for commercial plans. Medicare allows 180 days for reconsideration. Check your denial letter for the exact date and calendar it immediately.

Should I add new documentation at the second level?

Yes. Second-level appeals succeed more often when you add documentation not included in the first submission — physician attestation letters, peer-reviewed literature, corrected billing information, or authorization records. Simply resubmitting the same package rarely changes the outcome.
