Appeal Process

What Is an Appeal Deadline?

Miss an appeal deadline by even one day and a winnable denial becomes permanent revenue loss — most payers allow 30 to 180 days, but each level of review carries its own countdown clock.

Definition

The time limit within which a provider or patient must submit an appeal after receiving a denial — typically 30 to 180 days depending on the payer.

What an Appeal Deadline Means

An appeal deadline is the last date you can submit a formal appeal to challenge a payer's denial or payment reduction. It is a hard cutoff defined by the payer's contract, provider manual, or applicable state and federal regulations. The clock typically starts when the denial is issued on a remittance advice or when you receive a formal denial letter — not when you first discover the denial in your work queue.

Each level of the appeal process has its own deadline. The first-level window is usually the longest. Second-level and external review windows are shorter and begin when the prior level denial is issued.

Why Appeal Deadlines Matter

Appeal deadlines are the single most common reason recoverable denials become permanent write-offs. Billing teams that batch denial work weekly or monthly routinely miss 180-day windows on claims denied early in the cycle. A $800 denial with strong overturn potential becomes uncollectible the day after the deadline passes — no amount of clinical documentation changes that outcome.

Payers enforce deadlines strictly. Unlike clinical arguments where evidence can compensate for weak initial submissions, late appeals are rejected administratively without review of the merits.

How Appeal Deadlines Affect Your Claim

Meeting the deadline preserves your right to full appeal escalation — first level, second level, external review, and IMR where applicable. Missing it closes every structured path to recovery simultaneously. The claim balance typically transfers to bad debt or patient responsibility depending on the denial type and contractual adjustment group. For Medicare claims, missing the 120-day redetermination window requires an entirely different exception process with low success rates.

Calendar the appeal deadline the day you receive the remittance advice — not the day you start working the denial. Build your appeal letter and documentation before the deadline, not on it.

What to Do About Appeal Deadlines

Extract the appeal deadline from every remittance advice and denial letter at the point of posting. Enter it into your practice management or denial tracking system with automated alerts at 30, 14, and 7 days before expiration. Prioritize appeals by deadline first, dollar amount second. Generate appeal letters quickly — speed to submission matters more than perfection when the deadline is approaching. Submit with proof of delivery and retain the confirmation. When a first-level appeal is denied, immediately calendar the next-level deadline from the new denial letter.

Frequently Asked Questions

Where do I find my appeal deadline?

Check your remittance advice, denial letter, or Explanation of Benefits for the appeal filing deadline. Payer provider portals also list appeal timeframes in the appeals section. If no deadline is stated, consult the payer's provider manual or call provider services to confirm before submitting.

Does the appeal deadline start from the denial date or date of service?

Appeal deadlines typically start from the date the denial was issued or the date you received the remittance advice — not the date of service. Each payer defines the start date differently, so verify the trigger date in the payer's appeals policy.

What happens if I miss the appeal deadline?

Missing the appeal deadline usually means the denial becomes final and the payer will not reconsider the claim through the standard appeal process. In rare cases, you may request a retroactive appeal or file a complaint with the state insurance department, but success is unlikely without documented extenuating circumstances.

Do different appeal levels have different deadlines?

Yes. First-level appeal deadlines are often 60 to 180 days from the initial denial. Second-level and external review deadlines are typically shorter — 60 days or less from the prior level denial date. Each denial letter should state the deadline for the next level.
