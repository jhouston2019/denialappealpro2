Revenue Cycle

What Is Underpayment?

Not every revenue leak arrives as a denial — underpayments are quiet, partial shortfalls where the payer pays something but less than your contracted rate, and most practices never notice the difference.

Definition

When an insurer pays less than the contracted rate for a service — underpayments are appealable and represent a separate category of revenue leakage from outright denials.

What Underpayment Means

An underpayment occurs when a payer processes a claim and pays an amount below the contracted reimbursement rate for that service. Unlike a denial, where the payer refuses payment entirely, an underpayment means partial payment was made — which can make it harder to detect. Underpayments result from incorrect fee schedule application, wrong contract tier assignment, bundling errors that reduce the allowed amount, or payer processing mistakes. Each underpayment may be small — $15 to $50 per claim — but across thousands of claims annually, the cumulative loss is substantial. Underpayments are distinct from contractual adjustments, which reflect agreed-upon discounts rather than payer errors.

Why Underpayment Matters

Underpayments are the hidden tax on your revenue cycle. Because a payment was received, billing staff often post the remittance and move on without comparing the allowed amount to the contracted rate. Industry estimates suggest 2 to 5 percent of all paid claims contain some degree of underpayment. For a practice collecting $2 million annually from insurance, that represents $40,000 to $100,000 in recoverable revenue that most practices never pursue. Underpayment recovery requires contract rate knowledge, automated variance detection, and an appeal process — resources that many small and mid-size practices lack, making underpayments one of the most undermanaged revenue cycle problems.

How Underpayment Affects Your Practice

Underpayments erode your effective collection rate without triggering the alarms that denials do. Your denial rate may look healthy while underpayments silently reduce average reimbursement per claim. Payer contract negotiations suffer when you cannot demonstrate systematic underpayment patterns with data. Provider compensation models based on collections underpay clinicians when underpayments go unrecovered. Over time, accepting underpayments without challenge signals to payers that your contracted rates are soft targets — potentially leading to more aggressive payment reductions on future claims.

Underpayments and denials require different detection methods but the same recovery tool: a formal appeal with contract documentation. Do not assume partial payment means correct payment.

What to Do About Underpayments

Implement automated payment posting that flags any allowed amount below your contracted rate for review. Maintain current fee schedules for every payer contract and update them when rates change. Run a monthly underpayment report comparing expected versus actual reimbursement by payer and CPT code. File appeals for underpayments above a minimum threshold — typically $10 to $25 per claim — to ensure recovery efforts exceed administrative cost. Frequently Asked Questions

How do I identify an underpayment?

Compare the allowed amount on the remittance advice against your contracted fee schedule for that payer and service code. Any payment below the contracted rate — after accounting for legitimate adjustments — is an underpayment.

Can underpayments be appealed?

Yes. Underpayments are appealable when the payer paid less than the contracted rate without a valid adjustment reason. Submit an appeal with the contract rate documentation, the remittance advice showing the underpayment, and the original claim.

How is underpayment different from a denial?

A denial means the payer refused to pay for the service entirely. An underpayment means the payer paid something but less than the contracted amount. Denials are more visible; underpayments often go unnoticed because a partial payment was received.

How common are payer underpayments?

Industry data suggests that 2 to 5 percent of paid claims contain some degree of underpayment. Without automated payment variance detection, most practices recover less than half of the underpaid amounts they are owed.
