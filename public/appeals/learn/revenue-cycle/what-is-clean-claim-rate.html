Revenue Cycle

What Is Clean Claim Rate?

Your clean claim rate measures how often claims sail through on the first try — and every rejected claim that could have been caught upfront is a denial waiting to happen downstream.

Definition

The percentage of claims submitted that are accepted and processed without rejection or denial on first submission — top-performing practices achieve above 95 percent.

What Clean Claim Rate Means

Clean claim rate is a front-end revenue cycle metric that measures billing accuracy at the point of submission. A clean claim is one that payers accept without rejection, request for additional information, or denial on the first pass. The rate is calculated by dividing the number of first-pass accepted claims by total claims submitted over a given period. Unlike denial rate, which measures payer pushback after processing, clean claim rate captures whether your claims are structurally correct before they ever reach adjudication. It reflects the quality of your registration data, coding, charge capture, and pre-submission scrubbing.

Why Clean Claim Rate Matters

Every rejected claim costs your practice time and money. Staff must identify the rejection reason, correct the error, and resubmit — adding 14 to 30 days to the collection cycle. At scale, a 5 percent rejection rate on 1,000 monthly claims means 50 claims requiring rework every month. Industry benchmarks set the target at 95 percent or higher for well-managed practices. Falling below 90 percent signals that your front-end processes — eligibility checks, coding review, and claims editing — need immediate improvement. Improving clean claim rate is one of the highest-ROI investments in revenue cycle performance because it prevents problems rather than fixing them after the fact.

How Clean Claim Rate Affects Your Practice

A low clean claim rate creates a backlog of rework that pulls billing staff away from higher-value activities like denial appeals and underpayment review. Rejected claims inflate days in AR because they sit in limbo while corrections are made. Some rejected claims miss timely filing windows if rework takes too long, converting recoverable revenue into permanent write-offs. Clean claim rate also varies by payer — tracking it at the payer level reveals which insurance companies reject your claims most often and whether the issue is on your end or reflects payer-specific requirements you have not configured in your billing system.

Improving clean claim rate by just 3 percent can reduce your denial volume significantly — because many claims rejected at submission would have become denials after adjudication if they had been accepted initially.

What to Do About a Low Clean Claim Rate

Run a rejection reason report for the last 90 days and rank errors by frequency. The top 3 rejection causes typically account for more than 60 percent of all rejections. Implement automated claims scrubbing rules in your billing software to catch common errors before submission. Train front desk staff on accurate demographic and insurance data entry. Frequently Asked Questions

How is clean claim rate calculated?

Clean claim rate equals the number of claims accepted on first submission divided by total claims submitted, expressed as a percentage. Claims that are rejected, denied, or returned for correction on first pass count against the rate.

What is a good clean claim rate?

Top-performing practices achieve a clean claim rate above 95 percent. A rate below 90 percent indicates systemic issues in coding, eligibility verification, or claims scrubbing that need immediate attention.

What causes a low clean claim rate?

Common causes include incorrect patient demographics, missing or invalid NPI numbers, coding errors, lack of prior authorization, and submitting to the wrong payer. Each rejected claim adds rework cost and delays payment by 14 to 30 days.

How does clean claim rate relate to denials?

A low clean claim rate often predicts a high denial rate. Claims rejected at submission may be corrected and resubmitted, but claims that pass submission and are then denied require a full appeal process — which is more costly and time-consuming.
