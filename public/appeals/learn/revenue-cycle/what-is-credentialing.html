What Is Credentialing?

Before a provider can bill a single claim, they must be verified, enrolled, and approved by each payer — and credentialing gaps are one of the most preventable causes of denials for new providers.

Definition

The process of verifying a provider's qualifications and enrolling them with insurance plans — credentialing gaps are a common cause of claim denials for new providers.

What Credentialing Means

Credentialing is the process by which insurance payers verify a healthcare provider's qualifications before allowing them to participate in a network and bill for services. It involves confirming medical education, board certification, state licensure, DEA registration, malpractice insurance, work history, and any disciplinary actions. Once verified, the provider is enrolled with the payer and assigned effective dates for billing. Credentialing is distinct from contracting — a provider can be credentialed but not yet contracted at agreed-upon rates, or contracted but still awaiting credentialing approval. Both steps must be complete before claims will be paid.

Why Credentialing Matters

Credentialing is the gateway to reimbursement. A provider who sees patients before credentialing is complete cannot bill insurance for those services — and claims submitted during the gap are denied. The credentialing process takes 60 to 120 days on average, meaning a new provider who starts seeing patients on day one may have 2 to 4 months of services at risk. For a provider generating $30,000 per month in charges, a 90-day credentialing delay represents $90,000 in claims that will be denied unless retroactive enrollment is available. Proactive credentialing management is one of the highest-impact revenue protection activities for growing practices.

How Credentialing Affects Your Practice

Credentialing gaps create a predictable denial pattern that billing teams often misdiagnose. Claims denied for provider-not-enrolled or not-credentialed reasons may be reworked repeatedly without resolving the underlying issue — the provider simply is not in the payer's system yet. New provider onboarding requires parallel tracking of credentialing status across every payer the practice accepts. Re-credentialing, required every 2 to 3 years by most payers, creates recurring risk if renewal deadlines are missed. Group practices adding locum tenens or part-time providers face additional complexity when billing under supervising physician credentials versus individual enrollment.

Start credentialing at least 120 days before a new provider's first patient date. Every day of credentialing delay is a day of services that may be denied and require retroactive recovery efforts.

What to Do About Credentialing-Related Denials

Maintain a credentialing tracker showing each provider's enrollment status with every payer, including effective dates and re-credentialing deadlines. Before a new provider sees patients, confirm they are credentialed and contracted with the payers your patients carry. For claims denied due to credentialing gaps, check whether the payer allows retroactive billing once enrollment is approved — many commercial payers do within a limited window. If the provider was credentialed but the claim listed wrong information, submit a corrected claim. Frequently Asked Questions

How long does provider credentialing take?

Credentialing typically takes 60 to 120 days depending on the payer and state. Medicare enrollment through PECOS can take 60 to 90 days, while commercial payers vary widely. Starting the process before a provider's start date is essential.

What happens if a provider bills before credentialing is complete?

Claims submitted before credentialing is finalized are typically denied with a provider-not-enrolled or not-credentialed reason code. Some payers allow retroactive billing once credentialing is approved, but this is payer-specific and not guaranteed.

What is the difference between credentialing and contracting?

Credentialing verifies a provider's qualifications — education, licensure, board certification, and malpractice history. Contracting is the separate step of negotiating reimbursement rates and signing a participation agreement with the payer.

Can credentialing denials be appealed?

Claims denied because a provider was not yet credentialed at the date of service may be rebilled once credentialing is complete if the payer allows retroactive enrollment. If the provider was credentialed but listed incorrectly on the claim, a corrected resubmission usually resolves the denial.
