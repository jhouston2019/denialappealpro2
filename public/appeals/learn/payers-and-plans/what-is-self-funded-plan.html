Payers and Plans

What Is a Self-Funded Plan?

Roughly 65% of covered workers are enrolled in self-funded employer plans where the company — not an insurance carrier — bears the financial risk for every claim paid or denied.

Definition

An employer health plan where the employer pays claims directly rather than paying premiums to an insurer — governed by ERISA not state insurance law.

What a Self-Funded Plan Means

A self-funded plan, also called a self-insured plan, is an employer-sponsored health benefit arrangement where the employer assumes direct financial responsibility for employee medical claims rather than purchasing fully insured coverage from an insurance company. The employer sets aside funds to pay claims as they occur and typically hires a third-party administrator to handle claims processing, provider networks, and member services. Stop-loss insurance protects the employer against catastrophic individual or aggregate claim costs. Self-funded plans are governed by ERISA, the federal Employee Retirement Income Security Act, which preempts most state insurance regulations and establishes specific appeal rights for plan participants.

Why a Self-Funded Plan Matters

Identifying self-funded plan status changes everything about how you respond to a denial. Self-funded plans are not subject to state insurance mandates, state external review programs, or state prompt-pay laws that apply to fully insured plans. Your appeal rights come from ERISA and the plan document, not state consumer protection statutes. The insurer name on the patient's ID card — Aetna, Cigna, Blue Cross — may be the TPA, not the entity holding financial risk. Providers who treat self-funded plan members without understanding ERISA appeal procedures may miss deadlines, pursue incorrect external review pathways, or fail to build the administrative record needed for potential federal litigation.

How a Self-Funded Plan Affects Your Claim

Claims submitted to self-funded plans are processed by the TPA using the employer's plan document and medical policies, which may differ from the TPA's fully insured product policies. Denial notices must include ERISA-required elements: the specific reason for denial, reference to plan provisions, and a description of appeal rights with deadlines. Self-funded plan appeals typically allow 180 days for first-level filing, longer than many state-regulated plans. Because ERISA preempts state law, beneficiaries cannot access state insurance department complaints or independent external review for most self-funded denials. The administrative appeal record becomes the evidentiary foundation for any subsequent ERISA lawsuit in federal court.

The insurer logo on an ID card does not tell you whether a plan is self-funded. Request the Summary Plan Description to confirm ERISA status before choosing your appeal strategy.

What to Do About a Self-Funded Plan Denial

Obtain the plan document and Summary Plan Description to understand coverage terms and ERISA appeal procedures. File your first-level appeal within the plan deadline — at minimum 180 days under ERISA — and include all clinical documentation and plan language citations in this submission. Submit all evidence during the administrative process because federal courts generally will not consider new evidence after appeals are exhausted. Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my plan is self-funded?

Check your Summary Plan Description or ask your employer's HR department. Self-funded plans often display a major insurer's name on the ID card but include language like 'administered by' rather than 'insured by.' The plan document will identify the employer as the plan sponsor.

Can I file a complaint with my state insurance department for a self-funded plan?

Generally no. Self-funded plans governed by ERISA are exempt from state insurance regulation. Your appeal pathway runs through the plan's internal process and potentially federal ERISA litigation, not state external review.

Who processes claims for a self-funded plan?

Most self-funded plans use a third-party administrator or the insurance company's administrative services to process claims. The TPA handles day-to-day claims operations but does not assume financial risk for payment.

What appeal deadline applies to self-funded plans?

ERISA requires self-funded plans to allow at least 180 days from the adverse benefit determination to file a first-level appeal. Check your plan document for the exact deadline, as some plans allow longer periods.
