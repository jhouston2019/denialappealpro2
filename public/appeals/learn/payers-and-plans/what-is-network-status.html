Payers and Plans

What Is Network Status?

Out-of-network claims are denied at rates 15% higher than in-network claims — network status is the single verification step that determines whether a health plan pays your claim at all.

Definition

Whether a provider has a contract with a specific health plan — in-network providers have negotiated rates while out-of-network claims face higher denial rates.

What Network Status Means

Network status describes whether a health care provider has a contractual agreement with a specific health insurance plan to deliver services at negotiated reimbursement rates. In-network providers have signed participation agreements that define allowed amounts, billing requirements, and claims submission rules for that payer's members. Out-of-network providers have no such contract and are not bound by the plan's fee schedule. A provider may be in-network with one plan product from a payer but out-of-network with another product from the same payer, making network verification plan-specific rather than payer-specific. Network status is determined at the time of service and recorded during claims adjudication.

Why Network Status Matters

Network status is the first gate in claim adjudication and the leading preventable cause of claim denials across all plan types. HMO plans typically deny out-of-network claims entirely except in emergencies. PPO plans pay out-of-network claims at reduced rates. Even within the same payer, a provider credentialed for one employer group's self-funded plan may not be in-network for another group's plan administered by the same carrier. Failing to verify network status before rendering care exposes providers to full financial risk and patients to surprise balance bills. Revenue cycle data consistently shows that network-related denials are among the most costly because they often result in zero payment rather than partial payment.

How Network Status Affects Your Claim

When a claim is submitted, the payer's system matches the rendering provider's NPI and tax ID against its network database for the member's specific plan. If no contract exists, the claim is flagged as out-of-network and adjudicated under out-of-network benefit rules — which may mean full denial, reduced payment, or higher patient cost-sharing depending on plan type. CARC codes for network-related denials include codes indicating non-covered charges and contractual obligations. The No Surprises Act provides federal protections limiting patient cost-sharing when out-of-network providers deliver emergency services or ancillary care at in-network facilities, but these protections do not guarantee full provider payment.

Verify network status for the specific plan product on every patient visit — not just the payer name. Network participation varies by employer group, plan type, and geographic region.

What to Do About a Network Status Denial

Confirm your network status with the payer as of the date of service, not the date the claim was processed. If you were in-network but the payer's system shows otherwise, submit proof of your participation agreement or credentialing effective date. For emergency or surprise billing scenarios, cite No Surprises Act protections and document that the patient had no reasonable ability to choose an in-network provider. File an appeal within the plan deadline with evidence of network status, emergency circumstances, or network inadequacy. Frequently Asked Questions

How do I check if a provider is in-network?

Use the payer's online provider directory or call the member services number on the insurance ID card. Verify network status for the specific plan product, not just the payer name, because network participation varies by plan.

Can network status change during a plan year?

Yes. Providers can terminate network contracts or join new networks at any time. A provider who was in-network at the time of service may appear out-of-network in the payer's system if the contract ended before the claim was processed.

What is a surprise billing situation related to network status?

Surprise billing occurs when a patient receives care at an in-network facility but is treated by an out-of-network provider, such as an anesthesiologist or emergency physician. Federal No Surprises Act protections limit patient cost-sharing in many of these scenarios.

Can I appeal a denial based on out-of-network status?

You can appeal if the provider was in-network at the time of service, the care was emergent, or the plan's network was inadequate. Provide evidence of network status at the date of service and document any failed attempts to find in-network alternatives.
