Payers and Plans

What Is Medicare Advantage?

More than half of all Medicare beneficiaries now enroll in Medicare Advantage plans — private insurers that layer their own prior authorization rules and network restrictions on top of federal Medicare benefits.

Definition

Private insurance plans that contract with Medicare to provide Part A and B benefits — they have their own prior auth requirements and denial patterns.

What Medicare Advantage Means

Medicare Advantage, also known as Medicare Part C, is an alternative way to receive Medicare benefits through private insurance companies approved by CMS. These plans must cover everything Original Medicare covers but can add supplemental benefits like dental, vision, and fitness programs. MA plans operate as HMOs, PPOs, or other managed care models with defined provider networks and utilization management programs. When a beneficiary enrolls in Medicare Advantage, the plan receives a capitated payment from CMS and assumes responsibility for delivering Part A and Part B benefits according to both Medicare law and the plan's own medical policies.

Why Medicare Advantage Matters

Medicare Advantage plans deny claims at higher rates than Original Medicare, particularly for prior authorization failures, out-of-network services, and medical necessity determinations. Many initial prior auth denials lack sufficient justification and are overturned on appeal, indicating that proactive authorization management is essential. Because MA plans function like commercial insurers with Medicare funding, they apply prior auth requirements to services that Original Medicare would pay without advance approval. Providers and patients who assume Medicare rules apply uniformly often encounter unexpected denials when the beneficiary is enrolled in an MA plan rather than fee-for-service Medicare.

How Medicare Advantage Affects Your Claim

Claims submitted to a Medicare Advantage plan are adjudicated against the plan's medical policy, provider network contracts, and prior authorization database — not directly against CMS NCDs and LCDs. A service that Original Medicare would cover may be denied by an MA plan for missing prior auth, out-of-network provider status, or plan-specific step therapy requirements. MA plans issue their own remittance advice with CARC codes, and appeal deadlines typically follow commercial-style timeframes of 60 days rather than Medicare's 120-day redetermination window. Identifying the specific MA plan and its parent organization is essential because denial patterns vary significantly between carriers like Humana, UnitedHealthcare, and Kaiser Permanente.

Always verify whether your patient has Original Medicare or Medicare Advantage before submitting a claim. The payer ID, prior auth requirements, and appeal process differ completely between the two.

What to Do About a Medicare Advantage Denial

Confirm the beneficiary's MA plan name and obtain the plan's medical policy for the denied service. If the denial cites missing prior authorization, check whether retroactive auth is available and gather documentation showing the service was emergent or medically necessary. File a first-level appeal with the MA plan within 60 days, citing Medicare coverage requirements and plan-specific policy language. Request an expedited appeal if the denied service is urgent or ongoing. Frequently Asked Questions

Is Medicare Advantage the same as a Medicare Supplement?

No. Medicare Advantage replaces Original Medicare Parts A and B with a private plan. A Medicare Supplement, or Medigap, works alongside Original Medicare to cover copays and deductibles. They are mutually exclusive — you cannot have both.

Do Medicare Advantage plans require prior authorization?

Yes. Medicare Advantage plans commonly require prior authorization for specialist visits, advanced imaging, durable medical equipment, and many outpatient procedures. Missing prior auth is one of the top denial reasons for MA plans.

How do I appeal a Medicare Advantage denial?

File a first-level appeal directly with your Medicare Advantage plan within 60 days of the denial. If unsuccessful, you can request reconsideration, then escalate to an Independent Review Entity and ultimately an ALJ hearing.

Can a Medicare Advantage plan deny something Original Medicare covers?

Medicare Advantage plans must cover all Medicare-covered services, but they can impose additional requirements like prior authorization and network restrictions. A denial for failure to obtain prior auth may be overturned on appeal even if the service itself is covered.
