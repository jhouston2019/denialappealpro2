Payers and Plans

What Is Commercial Insurance?

Commercial insurance covers roughly 180 million Americans through employer group plans and individual policies — and its denial patterns, appeal deadlines, and regulatory framework differ sharply from government programs.

Definition

Private health insurance provided by employers or purchased individually — governed by state law or ERISA depending on plan type.

What Commercial Insurance Means

Commercial insurance refers to private health coverage sold by insurance companies rather than administered by federal or state government agencies. Employer-sponsored group plans represent the largest segment, where companies contract with insurers like Blue Cross, Aetna, or UnitedHealthcare to provide benefits to employees and dependents. Individual commercial policies are purchased directly through the ACA marketplace or off-exchange. These plans establish their own provider networks, fee schedules, prior authorization requirements, and medical necessity criteria that determine whether a claim is paid, reduced, or denied.

Why Commercial Insurance Matters

Understanding that your coverage is commercial insurance tells you which regulatory rules apply and which appeal pathways are available. Fully insured commercial plans must comply with state insurance mandates, prompt-pay laws, and external review requirements. Self-funded commercial plans fall under ERISA and follow different appeal procedures. Commercial payers account for the majority of claim denials in outpatient and specialty settings, and their denial reason codes, filing deadlines, and documentation requirements vary significantly from Medicare or Medicaid. Knowing your payer type is the foundation of an effective appeal strategy.

How Commercial Insurance Affects Your Claim

When you submit a claim to a commercial payer, the insurer applies its contract terms, medical policy, and utilization management rules before issuing payment. Common commercial denial triggers include missing prior authorization, out-of-network provider status, medical necessity determinations, and benefit exclusions listed in the plan document. Commercial payers typically use CARC codes on remittance advice to communicate denial reasons, and appeal deadlines range from 30 to 180 days depending on the plan and state. A single misidentified payer type can send your appeal down the wrong regulatory pathway and cost weeks of recovery time.

Before appealing a commercial denial, confirm whether your plan is fully insured or self-funded. The payer name on your ID card may be an administrator, not the entity that holds financial risk — and that distinction determines your legal rights.

What to Do About a Commercial Insurance Denial

Request your Explanation of Benefits and identify the payer, plan type, and denial reason code. Pull your Summary Plan Description and the payer's medical policy for the denied service. File a first-level appeal within the plan's deadline, citing specific plan language and clinical documentation that supports medical necessity. If the internal appeal fails, determine whether you qualify for external review through your state insurance department or an independent review organization. Frequently Asked Questions

Is commercial insurance the same as private insurance?

Yes. Commercial insurance and private insurance refer to the same category — health coverage sold by private companies rather than government programs like Medicare or Medicaid. This includes employer-sponsored group plans and individual marketplace policies.

Does commercial insurance follow state or federal law?

It depends on the plan type. Fully insured commercial plans are regulated by state insurance departments. Self-funded employer plans are governed by ERISA, a federal law that preempts most state regulations.

How long do commercial payers take to process appeals?

Most commercial insurers must respond to first-level appeals within 30 days for pre-service denials and 60 days for post-service denials under ACA requirements. Urgent care appeals must be decided within 72 hours.

Can I appeal a commercial insurance denial?

Yes. All ACA-compliant commercial plans must provide an internal appeal process and access to external review for certain denials. You typically have 180 days from the denial date to file a first-level appeal.
