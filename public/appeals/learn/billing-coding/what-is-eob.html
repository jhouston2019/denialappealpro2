Billing and Coding

What Is an EOB?

The Explanation of Benefits is the Rosetta Stone of claim adjudication — it translates payer decisions into billed amounts, allowed amounts, payments, and the codes that explain every adjustment.

Definition

The document sent by an insurer after processing a claim that shows what was billed, what was allowed, what was paid, and what the patient owes.

What an EOB Means

An EOB, or Explanation of Benefits, is a statement issued by an insurance company after a healthcare claim has been processed. It itemizes each service on the claim, showing the provider's billed charge, the payer's allowed amount, the amount paid by insurance, and any remaining patient responsibility. EOBs also include adjustment reason codes — CARC and RARC codes — that explain why specific line items were reduced or denied. Despite the name, an EOB is not a bill. It is an informational document that both providers and patients use to understand how a claim was adjudicated.

Why EOBs Matter

Every denial appeal starts with the EOB. Without it, you cannot determine the denial reason, the allowed amount, or the deadline for filing an appeal. Revenue cycle teams process hundreds of EOBs weekly, and each one must be matched to the original claim, posted to the patient account, and routed for follow-up when payment falls short. Studies show that providers leave an estimated 3% of revenue uncollected simply because EOB data is misread or not acted upon within the appeal window. Reading EOBs accurately is the most practical skill in denial management.

How EOBs Affect Your Claim

The EOB is the final record of the payer's decision on your claim. The allowed amount on the EOB determines your contractual write-off. The CARC codes tell you whether to appeal, rebill, or bill the patient. The patient responsibility column drives your patient statement. If the EOB shows a zero allowed amount with CARC 50, the payer denied the claim for medical necessity and you have a limited window — often 30 to 180 days — to submit an appeal with supporting documentation. Ignoring or misinterpreting EOB data leads to missed appeal deadlines and permanent revenue loss.

Match every EOB to its original claim within 48 hours of receipt. Delayed posting is the leading cause of missed appeal deadlines and incorrect patient billing.

What to Do When You Receive an EOB

When an EOB arrives, verify the patient name, date of service, and procedure codes against your charge record. Check the allowed amount against your contracted fee schedule. Review every CARC code on denied or reduced lines and route the claim to the appropriate work queue. Post payments and adjustments to the patient account promptly. Frequently Asked Questions

Is an EOB the same as a bill?

No. An EOB is an informational document from the insurer explaining how a claim was processed. A bill is a request for payment sent by the provider. Patients may receive both for the same service.

What is the difference between an EOB and an ERA?

An EOB is the human-readable version sent to patients. An Electronic Remittance Advice is the machine-readable 835 file sent to providers containing the same payment and adjustment data in HIPAA standard format.

Where are denial codes on an EOB?

Denial and adjustment codes appear in the remark or adjustment section of the EOB, typically as CARC codes with group codes like CO or PR next to each affected line item.

Should I appeal based on what the EOB says?

Yes. The EOB is the authoritative record of how the payer processed your claim. The CARC codes and allowed amounts on the EOB determine your appeal strategy and deadline.
