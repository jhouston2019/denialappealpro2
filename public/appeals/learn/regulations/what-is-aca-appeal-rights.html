Regulations and Law

What Are ACA Appeal Rights?

The Affordable Care Act guarantees every covered patient a structured path to challenge a denial — internal review first, then independent external review — with insurers required to respond within 30 to 60 days.

Definition

Protections under the Affordable Care Act requiring insurers to provide internal and external appeal processes for all denied claims.

What ACA Appeal Rights Mean

ACA appeal rights are federal protections established under the Affordable Care Act that require health insurers and group health plans to offer both internal and external review processes when a claim or prior authorization request is denied. These rights apply to most non-grandfathered plans and ensure that denials are not final decisions made unilaterally by the payer. Insurers must notify you of your appeal rights in writing at the time of denial and provide clear instructions for submitting an appeal.

Why ACA Appeal Rights Matter

Before the ACA, many plans offered limited or opaque appeal processes with no guarantee of independent review. ACA appeal rights created a uniform federal floor requiring meaningful review at multiple levels. For providers and patients, these protections mean that a denial is a procedural step rather than a permanent outcome — and that insurers face regulatory consequences for failing to offer compliant appeal procedures or missing mandated response deadlines.

How ACA Appeal Rights Affect Your Claim

When a claim is denied on an ACA-compliant plan, you have the right to submit a first-level internal appeal with supporting documentation. If the internal appeal is denied or the payer fails to respond within 30 days for pre-service denials or 60 days for post-service denials, you may request external review by an independent organization. External review decisions are binding on the insurer in most states, and a favorable ruling requires the payer to reprocess and pay the claim. Industry data shows that external reviewers overturn approximately 50 percent of denials sent for independent evaluation.

ACA appeal rights include expedited review for urgent cases — insurers must decide within 72 hours when delay would seriously jeopardize life or health. Request expedited review in writing when the clinical situation warrants it.

What to Do About a Denial Under ACA Rights

Read your denial letter carefully for appeal instructions, deadlines, and the specific reason cited. Submit a first-level appeal with clinical documentation that directly addresses the denial reason before the stated deadline. Track response dates — if the payer misses the 30-day or 60-day window, you may qualify for external review even before exhausting all internal levels. Frequently Asked Questions

Which plans must offer ACA appeal rights?

Most non-grandfathered health plans sold on or after March 23, 2010 must comply with ACA appeal requirements. This includes individual marketplace plans and most fully insured employer group plans. Grandfathered plans and certain self-funded ERISA plans follow different rules.

What is the difference between internal and external ACA appeals?

Internal appeals are reviewed by the insurance company through its own appeal process. External appeals are conducted by an independent review organization after internal appeals are exhausted or when the payer misses required response deadlines.

How quickly must insurers respond to ACA appeals?

For standard internal appeals, insurers must respond within 30 days for pre-service denials and 60 days for post-service denials. Expedited urgent appeals must be decided within 72 hours when waiting would seriously jeopardize life or health.

Do ACA appeal rights apply to providers?

Providers can appeal on behalf of patients when they have written authorization or when state law grants direct provider appeal rights. Many revenue cycle teams file first-level appeals for denied claims using the same ACA-mandated processes available to patients.
