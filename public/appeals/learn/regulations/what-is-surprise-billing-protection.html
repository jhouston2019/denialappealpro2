Regulations and Law

What Is Surprise Billing Protection?

The No Surprises Act took effect January 1, 2022 — banning balance billing for emergency and certain in-network facility services and creating a federal payment framework for out-of-network providers who previously relied on patient bills to close the gap.

Definition

Federal protections under the No Surprises Act preventing patients from being billed beyond in-network cost-sharing for certain out-of-network services.

What Surprise Billing Protection Means

Surprise billing protection refers to the federal safeguards established by the No Surprises Act that prohibit out-of-network providers and facilities from billing insured patients for amounts beyond the in-network cost-sharing for qualifying services. These protections apply to emergency services at any facility, non-emergency services at in-network facilities when an out-of-network provider is involved, and air ambulance transport. The law also establishes a payment standard and independent dispute resolution process for determining how much the insurer must pay the out-of-network provider.

Why Surprise Billing Protection Matters

Before the No Surprises Act, patients frequently received unexpected bills of $1,000 to $10,000 or more from out-of-network providers who participated in their care at in-network facilities without patient knowledge. Providers who were out-of-network often depended on balance billing patients to make up the gap between insurer payment and billed charges. The federal law shifted the payment dispute from the patient to a negotiation between the provider and insurer, with independent dispute resolution as the backstop. Providers must now pursue proper claim payment and IDR rather than sending surprise bills to patients.

How Surprise Billing Protection Affects Your Claim

When a protected service is rendered out-of-network, the provider must bill the insurer and accept the in-network cost-sharing amount from the patient — no more. The insurer pays the out-of-network provider at the median in-network rate for the geographic area. If the provider disputes the payment amount, the parties enter a 30-day open negotiation period followed by independent dispute resolution if no agreement is reached. Claims that are improperly processed as out-of-network when surprise billing protections apply should be appealed or corrected to ensure the provider receives the qualifying payment rate rather than an out-of-network benefit level denial.

Out-of-network providers at in-network facilities cannot balance bill patients for protected services. Your recovery path is proper claim submission, open negotiation with the payer, and independent dispute resolution — not patient billing.

What to Do When Surprise Billing Rules Apply

Verify whether the service qualifies for No Surprises Act protection — emergency care, services at an in-network facility with an out-of-network provider, or air ambulance. Submit the claim to the patient's insurer with appropriate billing codes and documentation. If payment is below the median in-network rate, initiate the 30-day open negotiation period with the payer. If negotiation fails, file for independent dispute resolution through the federal IDR portal. Do not balance bill the patient for protected services — violations carry penalties up to $10,000 per incident. Frequently Asked Questions

What services are covered by surprise billing protection?

The No Surprises Act protects patients from balance billing for emergency services at out-of-network facilities, non-emergency services at in-network facilities performed by out-of-network providers, and air ambulance services. Ground ambulance is not currently covered by the federal law.

How are out-of-network providers paid under the No Surprises Act?

Out-of-network providers are paid the median in-network rate for the same service in the same geographic area, or an amount determined through an independent dispute resolution process if the parties cannot agree on payment within 30 days.

Can out-of-network providers balance bill patients?

For protected services under the No Surprises Act, out-of-network providers and facilities may not bill patients for amounts beyond the in-network cost-sharing — deductible, copay, and coinsurance. Violations can result in penalties up to $10,000 per violation.

Does surprise billing protection apply to self-pay patients?

The No Surprises Act primarily protects insured patients from balance billing beyond in-network cost-sharing. Uninsured and self-pay patients receive separate protections including good faith estimate requirements and a patient-provider dispute resolution process for bills exceeding estimates by $400 or more.
