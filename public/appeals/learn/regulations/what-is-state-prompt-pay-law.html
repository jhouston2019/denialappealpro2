Regulations and Law

What Is a State Prompt Pay Law?

When a clean claim sits unpaid past the state deadline — often 30 days for electronic submissions — prompt pay laws give providers leverage beyond the appeal letter, including statutory interest on every day of delay.

Definition

State regulations requiring insurers to process and pay clean claims within a specified timeframe — typically 30 to 45 days.

What a State Prompt Pay Law Means

State prompt pay laws are statutes enacted by individual states requiring health insurers to adjudicate and pay clean claims within a defined number of days after receipt. Most states set separate timelines for electronic and paper claims, with electronic claims typically due within 30 days and paper claims within 45 days. If an insurer fails to pay or deny within the statutory window, the claim may be deemed accepted and the insurer becomes liable for interest penalties on the overdue amount.

Why Prompt Pay Laws Matter

Delayed payment is one of the largest hidden costs in revenue cycle management, tying up working capital and inflating days in accounts receivable. Prompt pay laws give providers a regulatory basis to demand timely adjudication rather than waiting indefinitely for payer action. When an insurer misses the deadline, providers in many states can collect statutory interest of 1 to 2 percent per month — turning a delayed $10,000 claim into hundreds of dollars in penalty interest. More than 40 states have some form of prompt pay legislation on the books.

How Prompt Pay Laws Affect Your Claim

The prompt pay clock starts when the payer receives a clean claim — one that requires no additional information to process. If the payer neither pays nor issues a formal denial within the statutory period, the claim may be automatically accepted under some state laws. Providers can file complaints with the state insurance department, which investigates chronic late payers and can impose fines. Prompt pay violations do not replace the need to appeal substantive denials, but they add financial pressure on payers who delay both payment and denial responses beyond legal limits.

Track the date your clean claim was received by the payer. If neither payment nor denial arrives within your state's statutory window, you may be entitled to interest penalties — document the submission date and follow up in writing.

What to Do When Prompt Pay Deadlines Are Missed

Confirm your claim was clean at submission and note the exact date the payer acknowledged receipt. Calculate the statutory deadline under your state's prompt pay law. Send a written demand for payment with interest to the payer's provider relations department citing the specific statute. If the payer still does not respond, file a complaint with your state insurance department. For substantive denials that arrive late, appeal the denial on its merits while separately pursuing interest penalties for the processing delay. Frequently Asked Questions

Which states have prompt pay laws?

More than 40 states have enacted some form of prompt pay legislation for health insurance claims. Requirements vary significantly by state — some mandate payment within 15 days for electronic claims while others allow up to 45 days for paper submissions.

What penalties apply for prompt pay violations?

Many state prompt pay laws impose interest penalties on late payments, typically 1 to 2 percent per month on the unpaid claim amount. Some states also assess flat penalties per late claim and allow providers to file complaints with the state insurance department.

Do prompt pay laws apply to self-funded employer plans?

Self-funded ERISA plans are generally exempt from state prompt pay laws due to ERISA preemption. State prompt pay requirements typically apply to fully insured commercial plans regulated by the state insurance department.

What counts as a clean claim under prompt pay rules?

A clean claim is one that can be processed without additional information from the provider — complete patient demographics, valid codes, required attachments, and no missing authorization numbers. The prompt pay clock starts when a clean claim is received.
