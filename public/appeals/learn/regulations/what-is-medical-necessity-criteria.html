Regulations and Law

What Are Medical Necessity Criteria?

When a payer denies with CARC code 50, they are applying their own clinical rulebook — and appeals that map your documentation to each criterion in that policy overturn denials at rates up to 60 percent.

Definition

The clinical standards and guidelines a payer uses to determine whether a service qualifies as medically necessary for coverage.

What Medical Necessity Criteria Mean

Medical necessity criteria are the clinical standards, evidence-based guidelines, and coverage policies a payer applies to decide whether a specific service is appropriate, effective, and required for a particular patient's condition. These criteria may reference diagnosis codes, prior treatment requirements, severity thresholds, frequency limits, and specific clinical findings that must be documented. Payers publish their criteria in clinical policy bulletins, coverage determination guidelines, and medical policy manuals that define when a service meets the definition of medically necessary under the plan.

Why Medical Necessity Criteria Matter

Medical necessity denials are the single largest denial category across commercial and Medicare claims, frequently appearing with CARC code 50. Understanding the payer's specific criteria before rendering a service prevents denials; understanding them after a denial is essential for a successful appeal. Each payer applies different criteria for the same procedure, meaning a service approved by one insurer may be denied by another based on different clinical thresholds. Revenue cycle teams that retrieve and reference the applicable policy in their appeal letters consistently achieve higher overturn rates than those submitting generic clinical notes without policy alignment.

How Medical Necessity Criteria Affect Your Claim

When a claim is processed, the payer's medical review system or clinical staff evaluates the billed service against the applicable medical necessity criteria. If the documentation on file or submitted with the claim does not demonstrate that the patient meets every criterion — such as failed conservative treatment, required diagnostic prerequisites, or specific symptom duration — the claim is denied. The remittance cites CARC code 50 or a related non-coverage code. The denial letter typically references the specific clinical policy number applied, which becomes the foundation for your appeal strategy.

Retrieve the payer's clinical policy cited in the denial and address every coverage criterion point by point in your appeal. Generic medical records without policy alignment are the primary reason medical necessity appeals fail.

What to Do About a Medical Necessity Denial

Locate the payer's clinical policy referenced in the denial letter and review every coverage indication and limitation. Compare your patient's clinical record against each criterion and identify gaps in documentation. File a first-level appeal with a letter that maps specific clinical findings to each policy requirement — failed conservative treatments, diagnostic test results, symptom duration, and specialist recommendations. Include peer-reviewed literature when the payer's criteria reference specific evidence standards. Frequently Asked Questions

Where do payers publish their medical necessity criteria?

Payers publish medical necessity criteria in clinical policy bulletins, coverage determination guidelines, and provider manuals available on their provider portal. Medicare publishes criteria through National and Local Coverage Determinations on the CMS Medicare Coverage Database.

What CARC code indicates a medical necessity denial?

CARC code 50 is the most common code for medical necessity denials, indicating the payer determined the service was not medically necessary. CARC code 151 may also appear when the payer determines the service is not covered under plan benefits.

Can medical necessity denials be overturned on appeal?

Yes. Medical necessity is the most appealed denial category and overturn rates range from 30 to 60 percent depending on payer and documentation quality. Successful appeals provide clinical evidence directly addressing each criterion in the payer's policy.

Do medical necessity criteria differ between payers?

Yes. Each payer maintains its own clinical policies, and criteria for the same procedure can differ significantly between commercial insurers and Medicare. Always reference the specific payer's policy that applied at the time of service.
