Regulations and Law

What Is ERISA?

If your health coverage comes through your employer, ERISA is the federal statute that defines how your plan must handle denied claims — and it covers roughly 136 million Americans with private group health benefits.

Definition

The Employee Retirement Income Security Act — the federal law governing most employer-sponsored health plans and establishing the right to appeal denied claims.

What ERISA Means

ERISA, the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974, is the federal framework that regulates employer-sponsored benefit plans, including the majority of group health plans in the United States. It sets minimum standards for plan administration, requires written plan documents, and mandates that participants receive a fair process when a claim is denied. Under ERISA, plan fiduciaries must act in the interest of participants and follow the terms of the plan document when making coverage decisions.

Why ERISA Matters

ERISA determines which appeal procedures apply to your denial and whether state insurance laws or federal courts have jurisdiction over your dispute. Self-funded employer plans are almost exclusively governed by ERISA, which preempts many state consumer protection laws. Understanding ERISA status tells you whether you can pursue state external review, file a complaint with a state insurance commissioner, or must follow federal ERISA appeal and litigation pathways instead.

How ERISA Affects Your Claim

When a claim is denied under an ERISA plan, the payer must issue a written adverse benefit determination explaining the reason, citing plan provisions, and describing your appeal rights. You typically have 180 days to file a first-level appeal and must submit all supporting evidence during the administrative process — federal courts generally will not consider new evidence after appeals are exhausted. ERISA plans must respond to appeals within specified timeframes, and failure to meet those deadlines can strengthen your position in a subsequent federal lawsuit.

ERISA preempts most state laws for employer-sponsored plans. Before pursuing state external review, confirm whether your plan is ERISA-governed — the wrong appeal pathway can delay recovery by months.

What to Do About an ERISA Denial

Request your Summary Plan Description and the full plan document to understand coverage terms and appeal procedures. File your first-level appeal within the plan deadline, include all clinical documentation and policy references, and keep copies of every submission. If the plan upholds the denial, consider a second-level appeal if available, then evaluate whether federal ERISA litigation is appropriate for the dollar amount involved. Frequently Asked Questions

Does ERISA apply to all health insurance plans?

ERISA applies to most employer-sponsored self-funded and fully insured group health plans. It does not cover individual marketplace plans, Medicare, Medicaid, or government employee plans. If your coverage comes through your employer's benefits program, ERISA likely governs your appeal rights.

What appeal rights does ERISA provide?

ERISA requires plan administrators to provide a full and fair review of denied claims. You must receive a written explanation of the denial, access to plan documents, and the opportunity to submit additional evidence before a final decision is made.

Can I sue my insurer under ERISA?

After exhausting internal appeals, you may file a federal ERISA lawsuit to challenge a final benefit denial. ERISA claims are typically limited to recovering the denied benefit amount plus attorney fees in some cases, rather than punitive damages available in state law claims.

How long do I have to appeal an ERISA denial?

ERISA plans must allow at least 180 days from the date of the adverse benefit determination to file a first-level appeal. Check your Summary Plan Description for exact deadlines, as missing them can permanently bar your claim.
