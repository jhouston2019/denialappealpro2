Regulations and Law

What Is EMTALA?

The Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act compels every Medicare-participating hospital to treat emergencies before asking about insurance — and that legal obligation shapes how payers must process the claims that follow.

Definition

The Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act — requires hospitals to provide emergency care regardless of ability to pay and affects how emergency claims must be processed.

What EMTALA Means

EMTALA, the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act of 1986, is a federal law requiring Medicare-participating hospitals with emergency departments to provide a medical screening examination and necessary stabilizing treatment to any individual who comes to the ER, regardless of insurance status or ability to pay. Hospitals cannot delay treatment to verify coverage, demand payment upfront, or transfer unstable patients unless specific EMTALA conditions for transfer are met. The law applies to the hospital and to physicians on call who provide emergency services.

Why EMTALA Matters

EMTALA creates a fundamental tension in emergency billing: hospitals must treat first and bill later, while insurers sometimes deny payment citing missing prior authorization or non-emergency classification after the fact. Understanding EMTALA strengthens appeal arguments when payers deny ER claims for authorization failures on true emergencies. Revenue cycle teams that document the emergency nature of the visit at the time of service — not retroactively — recover significantly more on denied ER claims. CMS can impose civil monetary penalties of up to approximately $127,000 per EMTALA violation against non-compliant hospitals.

How EMTALA Affects Your Claim

When an emergency claim is submitted, payers may review whether the visit met their definition of a medical emergency and whether prior authorization requirements apply. True emergency services are generally covered benefits under most plans, and many state laws and the No Surprises Act prohibit authorization-based denials for genuine emergencies. If a payer classifies an ER visit as non-emergent and denies payment, the hospital can appeal by documenting that the presenting condition required immediate stabilization under EMTALA standards. Proper ER coding with condition codes and modifier usage also affects whether the claim is processed under emergency benefits.

EMTALA requires treatment regardless of authorization status. If a payer denies an ER claim for missing prior authorization on a true emergency, cite EMTALA and applicable state emergency service protections in your appeal.

What to Do About an Emergency Claim Denial

Review the ER record for documentation of the presenting complaint, vital signs, physician assessment, and stabilization actions taken. Confirm the claim was billed with appropriate emergency service codes and place-of-service indicators. If the denial cites lack of authorization or non-emergency classification, file an appeal with the ER physician note demonstrating that the condition required immediate evaluation and stabilization. Frequently Asked Questions

Does EMTALA require insurers to pay emergency claims?

EMTALA requires hospitals to stabilize and treat emergency patients regardless of insurance status. It does not directly mandate insurer payment, but emergency services are generally covered benefits and denials based on failure to obtain prior authorization for true emergencies are often appealable.

What qualifies as an EMTALA emergency?

An EMTALA emergency is a medical condition manifesting by acute symptoms of sufficient severity that the absence of immediate medical attention could reasonably be expected to result in serious health consequences, organ dysfunction, or death.

Can payers deny emergency room claims for lack of authorization?

Many states and the No Surprises Act prohibit insurers from denying payment for true emergency services based solely on failure to obtain prior authorization. If a payer denies an ER claim for authorization, appeal citing emergency service protections and EMTALA stabilization requirements.

Who enforces EMTALA violations?

The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services enforces EMTALA through hospital surveys and can terminate a hospital's Medicare provider agreement for violations. Civil monetary penalties up to approximately $127,000 per violation can be assessed against hospitals and physicians.
