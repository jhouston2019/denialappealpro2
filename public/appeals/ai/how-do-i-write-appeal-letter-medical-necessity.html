Medical Necessity Writing

How Do I Write an Appeal Letter for Medical Necessity?

CARC 50 denials reject your clinical judgment. Your appeal must quote the payer's own coverage criteria and show documented findings from the patient record meet every requirement listed.

The Direct Answer

Write a medical necessity appeal letter by quoting the payer's LCD or NCD coverage criteria, then matching each criterion to specific clinical findings documented in the patient record — diagnosis codes, test results, treatment history, and physician notes. CARC 50 medical necessity denials represent about 18% of all claim denials. Appeals that cite payer policy language alongside chart evidence have overturn rates above 55% at first level.

How It Works

Locate the payer's medical policy for the denied service. For Medicare, search the CMS Coverage Database for the applicable LCD or NCD. For commercial payers, check their provider portal for the medical policy document. Copy the specific coverage criteria into your appeal letter as numbered requirements.

For each criterion, cite the clinical evidence from the patient record that satisfies it — lab values, imaging results, prior treatment failures, specialist notes. Structure the letter as a point-by-point rebuttal rather than a general argument that the service was necessary. Attach the relevant progress notes, test results, and the payer policy excerpt. Frequently Asked Questions

What should a medical necessity appeal letter include?

Include the claim number, denied service, CARC 50 code, payer coverage criteria from the LCD or NCD, and point-by-point clinical evidence from the patient record showing each coverage requirement is met.

How do I find payer coverage criteria for my appeal?

Search the CMS Medicare Coverage Database for LCDs and NCDs, or check the payer's medical policy portal for commercial plans. Quote the specific policy number and criteria language in your appeal letter.

Which CARC code indicates a medical necessity denial?

CARC 50 is the primary medical necessity denial code, meaning the payer determined the service was not medically necessary for this patient. CARC 151 and CARC 167 also relate to coverage and medical necessity determinations.
