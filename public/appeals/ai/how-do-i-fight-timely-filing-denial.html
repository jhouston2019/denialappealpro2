The Direct Answer

Fight a timely filing denial by submitting an appeal letter with clearinghouse acceptance timestamps proving your claim was originally filed within the payer's deadline — typically 90 to 365 days from date of service depending on the plan. How It Works

Pull your clearinghouse submission report for the original claim and identify the acceptance timestamp. Compare it against the payer's timely filing limit and the date of service on the denied claim. If the submission date falls within the filing window, your appeal argument is straightforward: the claim was filed on time and the payer's denial is incorrect.

Write your appeal citing the CARC 29 code, the payer's filing deadline, your actual submission date from clearinghouse records, and attach the 277CA acknowledgment, clearinghouse report, and original claim. Frequently Asked Questions

What is a timely filing denial?

A timely filing denial means the payer claims your claim was submitted after their filing deadline — typically 90 to 365 days from the date of service. CARC 29 is the standard code for this denial type.

What proof do I need to overturn a timely filing denial?

Clearinghouse acceptance reports showing the original submission date within the filing window are the strongest evidence. Also include 277CA acknowledgment files, payer portal submission screenshots, or certified mail receipts if applicable.

Can I appeal a timely filing denial if I truly filed late?

Appeals succeed when you can prove the claim was actually filed on time but the payer lost or misprocessed it. If the claim was genuinely filed late, the appeal options are limited unless extenuating circumstances like payer system outages apply.
