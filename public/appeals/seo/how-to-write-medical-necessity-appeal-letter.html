Clinical Appeal Writing

How to Write a Medical Necessity Appeal Letter

When a payer denies your $3,500 MRI or $800 DME claim as not medically necessary, your appeal letter must translate clinical facts into coverage criteria language — not express frustration about the denial.

Start With the Payer's Own Coverage Policy

Before writing a word, pull the payer's medical policy or Local Coverage Determination for the denied CPT code. Every policy lists specific criteria that must be met for coverage. Your appeal letter should quote these criteria and address each one with a corresponding finding from the patient's medical record. This approach shifts the argument from your opinion to the payer's published standards.

Build the Clinical Narrative

Structure your letter around three clinical facts: what condition the patient has, what treatment was ordered and why, and what outcome the treatment is expected to produce. Pull specific data points from progress notes — lab values, imaging findings, symptom duration, failed prior treatments. Vague statements like the patient needed this service lose appeals. Specific findings like elevated A1C of 9.2 despite 6 months of metformin win them.

Quote the payer's LCD criteria and match each requirement to a documented clinical finding. This single technique overturns more medical necessity denials than any other approach.

Attach the Right Clinical Records

  • Progress notes from the ordering physician with diagnosis and treatment plan
  • Lab results, imaging reports, or pathology supporting the clinical decision
  • History of prior treatments attempted and their outcomes
  • Physician letter of medical necessity for high-dollar or DME claims

Close With a Specific Reprocessing Request

End your letter by requesting the payer reprocess the claim for payment based on the attached clinical evidence demonstrating medical necessity per their published coverage criteria. Include the claim number, billed amount, and your contact information. A direct, specific request gets a faster response than an open-ended plea for reconsideration.
