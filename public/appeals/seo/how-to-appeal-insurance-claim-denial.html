First-Level Appeal Process

How to Appeal an Insurance Claim Denial

Roughly 63% of first-level appeals result in partial or full payment. If you received a denial on a $500 or $5,000 claim, the appeal process is your direct path to recovering that revenue.

Step 1: Read Your EOB and Identify the Denial Code

Your Explanation of Benefits lists a CARC code that tells you exactly why the payer rejected your claim. Code 50 means medical necessity. Code 29 means timely filing. Code 97 means bundling. Before you write a single word of your appeal, match the denial reason to the documentation you will need. Pull the original claim, the remittance advice, and any authorization records tied to that date of service.

Step 2: Confirm Your Appeal Deadline

Most commercial payers give you 180 days from the denial date to file a first-level appeal. Medicare allows 120 days. Missing this window means the payer keeps your money permanently. Mark the deadline on your work queue the day the denial hits your desk — not the day you finally get around to researching it.

Step 3: Gather Supporting Documentation

Your appeal letter is only as strong as the records attached to it. For a medical necessity denial, attach progress notes, lab results, and physician orders. For a timely filing denial, attach proof of original submission with timestamps. For an authorization denial, attach the precert number or retro auth approval. Every attachment should directly refute the CARC code cited on the denial.

Payers deny claims for predictable, code-specific reasons. An appeal that addresses the exact CARC code on your EOB outperforms a generic letter every time.

Step 4: Write and Submit Your Appeal Letter

Your appeal letter should state the patient name, claim number, date of service, denial reason, and a direct rebuttal with supporting evidence. Reference specific clinical findings or billing records — not general statements about the service being appropriate. Submit through the payer's portal, fax, or mail as specified in your denial letter. Keep confirmation of submission with a date stamp.

Step 5: Track the Outcome and Escalate if Needed

First-level appeals typically receive a response within 30 to 60 days. If denied again, you have the right to a second-level or external review depending on your plan type. Document every submission and response. Practices that track appeal outcomes recover an average of $47,000 more per year in previously denied claims.
